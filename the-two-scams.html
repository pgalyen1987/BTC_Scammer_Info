What Happened

The victim's entire BTC balance was drained in one transaction. The 41-output fan-out pattern is a known laundering technique: splitting stolen funds across many addresses simultaneously to complicate tracing. An intermediary aggregator wallet (34HVDP9RFA9MpopGQ4TutLLVMFhhJFM9AX) collected these fragments and forwarded 97.25 BTC to a confirmed Binance hot wallet across 34 transactions — meaning Binance holds KYC identity records for the account that received the stolen funds.

What We Learned from Scam 1

Blockchain tracing identified the master scammer wallet and the Binance cash-out path, but the scam itself left few investigable leads beyond the blockchain. The scammer wallet's enormous scale (395,190 BTC lifetime) told us this was a large operation, but we had no corporate names, domains, or infrastructure to investigate further. That changed with Scam 2.

Source code exposed WeChat handles bmate601 / bizzzan01 and Shanghai ICP 沪ICP备13026899号-3. Full corporate table (director address, MSB #, branch): Corporate Network.

The Blockchain Connection: One Wallet, Two Thefts

CRITICAL FINDING — Same Criminal Organization Behind Both Scams
Independent of the OSINT trail above, blockchain analysis proved the two scams are connected at the wallet level:
  • Scam 1 (2022): Victim's stolen 0.848 BTC reached scammer wallet 3GH4EhMi1MG8rxSiAWqfoiUCMLaWPTCxuy via a 2-hop relay chain.
  • Scam 2 (July 2024): The deptoy.co/BIPPAX aggregation wallet sent 140 BTC in 7 direct transactions to that same scammer wallet.

Only the holder of the private key for this wallet can receive funds at that address. The same person or group stole from this victim in 2022 and again in July 2024 through a different platform. This means the OSINT trail uncovered from Scam 2 (deptoy.co → BIPPAX → Dorfman → Nanolite → CTG Server → SHARE-DNS) also applies to Scam 1.
